  01 UNIT

Introduction to Research Methods. The Scientific Investigation and Ethics in Computing

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 1, I explored research methods, focusing on the scientific method and ethics in computing. We examined the importance of research and its three main purposes: to explore, describe, and explain. A key focus was on inductive and deductive reasoning, which are essential approaches for problem-solving in research. Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to specific conclusions, while inductive reasoning builds general theories based on observations. Understanding these concepts is crucial for conducting structured and ethical research.
As part of the e-Portfolio activities, I started working on Reflective Activity 1 – Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI, which helped me consider the ethical implications of emerging technologies. I also engaged in Collaborative Learning Discussion 1, where I chose a case study on UX Dark Patterns, exploring the ethical concerns surrounding deceptive design practices in user interfaces. Completing the Reasoning Quiz (Quiz results), (Quiz Summary) reinforced my understanding of different reasoning approaches. Additionally, I attended the Unit 1 Seminar, which provided an overview of the module structure, key concepts, and research topics. For my research project, I selected "Implementing Machine Learning Tools and/or Techniques in Diabetes Diagnosis", a topic that interests me due to its real-world applications in healthcare. My interest in this area stems from my Master’s thesis at the University for the Creative Arts, where I worked on "Non-Invasive Glucose Monitoring (NIGM) System: Designing a Sustainable Solution for Diabetes Self-Management Using the Design Thinking Process." This research focused on developing innovative, patient-centered approaches to diabetes care, and I am eager to explore how Machine Learning can further enhance diagnostic and management solutions for this condition.

  03 UNIT

Methodology and Research Methods

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 3, I explored research methodology and research methods, focusing on the underlying assumptions that shape research design. Understanding ontological, epistemological, and axiological assumptions is crucial, as they influence how research questions are framed, the methods selected, and the interpretation of findings. I examined exploratory and conclusive research designs, where exploratory research helps define ambiguous problems, while conclusive research provides definitive answers. Additionally, I learned about qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-method research, each offering distinct approaches to data collection and analysis. Qualitative research, often associated with inductive reasoning, captures subjective experiences through case studies, interviews, and observations, while quantitative research employs statistical methods to establish patterns and relationships through numerical data. Mixed-method research integrates both approaches, providing a comprehensive analysis suited to complex research questions.
As part of my e-Portfolio activities, I conducted a peer review of two research papers of (Article 1) and (Article 2) in computing, assessing how different methodologies shaped their findings. This exercise enhanced my ability to critically evaluate research design and methodology. I also refined e-Portfolio Activity on Research Proposal Review, ensuring alignment between my research objectives and the appropriate data collection methods. As well worked on the Literature List. Furthermore, I participated in the Unit 3 seminar, engaging in discussions on the role of peer review in evaluating existing literature. Additionally, I completed my Collaborative Learning Discussion 1 Summary Post, incorporating feedback from my peers to reflect on my initial discussion.
This unit deepened my understanding of how research methodology influences the validity and reliability of findings. Selecting an appropriate research approach is critical to producing meaningful and ethical research. These insights will be instrumental in shaping my study on machine learning in diabetes diagnosis, as choosing the right research methods will determine the accuracy and applicability of my findings.

  04 UNIT

Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 4, I explored various data collection methods, including case studies, focus groups, and both qualitative and quantitative observations, each offering unique perspectives for research. Case studies allow for in-depth exploration of individuals or groups, fostering hypothesis development but lacking predictive accuracy and generalisability. Focus groups, with carefully selected participants sharing common backgrounds, enable dynamic discussions to answer complex questions about motivations and behaviours. Quantitative observation focuses on measurable data, such as size, volume, or frequency, while qualitative observation captures descriptive characteristics from a distance, ensuring that natural behaviours remain unaffected by the presence of the researcher.
This unit's activities included preparation for a seminar: Case Study on Privacy focused on privacy considerations in data collection, where ethical issues often arise. I also submitted my Literature Review Outline and received valuable formative feedback that guided me in refining my research approach. The assigned readings provided a comprehensive understanding of the advantages and limitations of each data collection method, reinforcing the importance of aligning methods with specific research objectives.
A key takeaway from this unit was the realisation that combining multiple methods can produce more comprehensive and reliable research outcomes. For example, integrating qualitative observations with focus groups provides both rich contextual data and insights into participants' perspectives. Similarly, blending case studies with quantitative observation offers a balance between detailed descriptions and objective data measurement. Understanding this nuanced approach has equipped me with essential skills for conducting structured and ethically sound research, fostering a more critical evaluation of methods suited for different types of studies.

  05 UNIT

Interviews, Survey Methods, and Questionnaire Design

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 5, I explored interviews, surveys, and questionnaire design, gaining insights into their distinct roles in qualitative and quantitative research. Interviews emerged as invaluable tools for gathering detailed and personalised insights, allowing researchers to probe deeper into respondents' experiences. Their conversational nature contrasts sharply with surveys, which excel in capturing broader trends and opinions across larger populations. An interesting distinction became evident: while surveys involve a comprehensive process of data collection and analysis, questionnaires are simply structured instruments used within surveys to facilitate data gathering.
One of the most valuable learning points was the introduction of pre- and post-testing techniques. These methods offer researchers a "before-and-after" framework, making them essential for evaluating the effectiveness of processes or interventions. Additionally, the distinction between open-ended questions used for qualitative insights and closed-ended questions for quantitative validation underscored the importance of question design in achieving meaningful research outcomes.
As part of the unit activities, I completed Reflective Activity 2, where I evaluated suitable data collection tools for my ongoing research project, considering how interviews could capture detailed experiences while surveys could validate trends. This exercise prompted me to explore a mixed-methods approach for more comprehensive insights. The Wiki activity (post, original questionnaire, improved questionnaire) on questionnaire design was particularly enlightening, as it highlighted the dangers of poorly structured questionnaires and provided strategies to craft effective ones that avoid ambiguity and capture accurate data.
Overall, this unit reinforced the importance of selecting appropriate data collection methods based on research objectives. By combining qualitative depth with quantitative validation, I am now better equipped to design robust research instruments and analyse data effectively to support sound, evidence-based conclusions.

  06 UNIT

Quantitative Methods - Descriptive and Inferential Statistics

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 6, I explored quantitative methods, focusing on descriptive and inferential statistics. These methods play a crucial role in data-driven decision-making across scientific research and business applications. Descriptive statistics summarise data, helping to identify patterns and trends, while inferential statistics allow researchers to draw conclusions about a population based on sample data. The concepts of location (mean, median, mode) and dispersion (range, variance, standard deviation) were particularly insightful, as they provide foundational tools for understanding data distributions. This knowledge is essential in fields such as finance, healthcare, and marketing, where data-driven insights inform strategies and policies.
As part of the e-Portfolio activities, I engaged with Unit 6 reading materials. I also worked on the Seminar Preparation Questions for Unit 7, which involved applying descriptive statistics and hypothesis testing. These exercises deepened my understanding of how data is organised and analysed to extract meaningful insights. Preparing for the Inferential Statistics Workshop required me to critically evaluate different levels of measurement and their implications for statistical analysis. Through these activities, I have gained a stronger appreciation for the role of quantitative methods in turning raw data into actionable intelligence.

  07 UNIT

Inferential Statistics and Hypothesis Testing

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 7, I delved into inferential statistics and hypothesis testing, essential tools in data analysis. Inferential statistics allow researchers to draw conclusions about a population based on a sample, helping to identify trends and relationships. Unlike descriptive statistics, which summarise data, inferential methods use probability to make predictions and test hypotheses. Understanding probability distributions is crucial, as they determine the validity of statistical conclusions. For instance, normal distribution plays a key role in many inferential techniques, enabling us to estimate population parameters with confidence. Hypothesis testing, a fundamental aspect of this unit, involves evaluating assumptions about data through statistical significance. This process is widely used in scientific research, business analytics, and healthcare studies to validate findings and inform decision-making.
During this unit, I engaged in various e-Portfolio activities, including the Hypothesis Testing Worksheet (Exa 7.4F; Exa 7.6B) and Summary Measures Worksheet (Exa 7.1B; Exa 7.2B; Exa 7.3D), which required me to apply inferential techniques to real datasets. Completing these exercises helped me interpret statistical results critically. Additionally, I participated in Collaborative Learning Discussion 2. Preparing for seminar 7 Inferential Statistics Workshop further reinforced my ability to conduct hypothesis tests and evaluate statistical evidence effectively. These activities have strengthened my analytical skills, equipping me with a deeper understanding of how inferential statistics can transform raw data into meaningful conclusions. A significant milestone in this unit was the submission of my assessment on Literature Review, which required synthesising relevant academic sources to support my research.

  09 UNIT

Validity and Generalisability in Research

Required Reading & Reflection

Unit 9 explored the essential concepts of validity, generalisability, and reliability, which are crucial for robust research design. Validity assesses whether the research measures what it intends to, while reliability focuses on the consistency of the measurement process. A key insight was that reliability is a prerequisite for validity—a measurement cannot be valid if it is not reliable. This interplay highlights the importance of ensuring both consistency and accuracy in research.
The unit also contrasted qualitative and quantitative data in terms of validity and generalisability. Quantitative research often aims for generalisability, applying findings to broader populations, whereas qualitative research prioritises depth and context, often sacrificing generalisability for richer insights. This distinction emphasised the need to align research design with the type of data and research question.
I completed the Charts Worksheet, which involved analysing and visualising data to present findings effectively. This activity reinforced the importance of clear data presentation. I prepeared for the Unit 9 Seminar, where we discussed challenges in ensuring validity and reliability in both qualitative and quantitative research, encouraging critical thinking about research design. Additionally, I completed my Summary Post on Collaborative Learning Discussion 2, incorporating feedback from my peers to reflect on my initial discussion.
These activities have enhanced my understanding of research credibility, particularly for my proposal on machine learning in diabetes diagnosis. Unit 9 has equipped me with the tools to ensure my research is both valid and reliable, strengthening its impact.
