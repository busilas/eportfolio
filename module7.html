  01 UNIT

Introduction to Research Methods. The Scientific Investigation and Ethics in Computing

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 1, I explored research methods, focusing on the scientific method and ethics in computing. We examined the importance of research and its three main purposes: to explore, describe, and explain. A key focus was on inductive and deductive reasoning, which are essential approaches for problem-solving in research. Deductive reasoning moves from general principles to specific conclusions, while inductive reasoning builds general theories based on observations. Understanding these concepts is crucial for conducting structured and ethical research.
As part of the e-Portfolio activities, I started working on Reflective Activity 1 – Ethics in Computing in the Age of Generative AI, which helped me consider the ethical implications of emerging technologies. I also engaged in Collaborative Learning Discussion 1, where I chose a case study on UX Dark Patterns, exploring the ethical concerns surrounding deceptive design practices in user interfaces. Completing the Reasoning Quiz (Quiz results), (Quiz Summary) reinforced my understanding of different reasoning approaches. Additionally, I attended the Unit 1 Seminar, which provided an overview of the module structure, key concepts, and research topics. For my research project, I selected "Implementing Machine Learning Tools and/or Techniques in Diabetes Diagnosis", a topic that interests me due to its real-world applications in healthcare. My interest in this area stems from my Master’s thesis at the University for the Creative Arts, where I worked on "Non-Invasive Glucose Monitoring (NIGM) System: Designing a Sustainable Solution for Diabetes Self-Management Using the Design Thinking Process." This research focused on developing innovative, patient-centered approaches to diabetes care, and I am eager to explore how Machine Learning can further enhance diagnostic and management solutions for this condition.

  02 UNIT

Research Questions, the Literature Review and the Research Proposal

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 2, I explored the foundational aspects of formulating a research proposal, viewing it as the blueprint that guides the research process. A research proposal must be meticulously structured, beginning with defining a suitable research question. This process requires balancing creativity and rational thinking to ensure the research remains feasible and meaningful.
A crucial component of the proposal is the literature review, which provides a critical overview of existing knowledge. This exercise reinforced the importance of identifying relevant theories, methodologies, and research gaps. By analysing the required reading, I gained insights into conducting effective literature reviews within scientific disciplines.
As part of the e-Portfolio activities, I refined my Literature Review and Research Proposal Outlines for my literature review and research proposal. I started to work on the list of literature. Additionally, I engaged in peer discussions by responding to three contributions (peer response 1), (peer response 2), (peer response 3) from Unit 1. These discussions deepened my understanding by exposing me to different research approaches and interpretations. I also prepared for the upcoming seminar by attempting the preparatory questions for Unit 3.
This unit strengthened my ability to critically evaluate scholarly sources and construct a well-founded research proposal. The structural approach to research planning has provided me with a clearer roadmap for my academic inquiry.

  03 UNIT

Methodology and Research Methods

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 3, I explored research methodology and research methods, focusing on the underlying assumptions that shape research design. Understanding ontological, epistemological, and axiological assumptions is crucial, as they influence how research questions are framed, the methods selected, and the interpretation of findings. I examined exploratory and conclusive research designs, where exploratory research helps define ambiguous problems, while conclusive research provides definitive answers. Additionally, I learned about qualitative, quantitative, and mixed-method research, each offering distinct approaches to data collection and analysis. Qualitative research, often associated with inductive reasoning, captures subjective experiences through case studies, interviews, and observations, while quantitative research employs statistical methods to establish patterns and relationships through numerical data. Mixed-method research integrates both approaches, providing a comprehensive analysis suited to complex research questions.
As part of my e-Portfolio activities, I conducted a peer review of two research papers of (Article 1) and (Article 2) in computing, assessing how different methodologies shaped their findings. This exercise enhanced my ability to critically evaluate research design and methodology. I also refined e-Portfolio Activity on Research Proposal Review, ensuring alignment between my research objectives and the appropriate data collection methods. As well worked on the literature list. Furthermore, I participated in the Unit 3 seminar, engaging in discussions on the role of peer review in evaluating existing literature. Additionally, I completed my Unit 1 summary post, incorporating feedback from my peers to reflect on my initial discussion.
This unit deepened my understanding of how research methodology influences the validity and reliability of findings. Selecting an appropriate research approach is critical to producing meaningful and ethical research. These insights will be instrumental in shaping my study on machine learning in diabetes diagnosis, as choosing the right research methods will determine the accuracy and applicability of my findings.

  04 UNIT

Case Studies, Focus Groups and Observations

Required Reading & Reflection

In Unit 4, I explored various data collection methods, including case studies, focus groups, and both qualitative and quantitative observations, each offering unique perspectives for research. Case studies allow for in-depth exploration of individuals or groups, fostering hypothesis development but lacking predictive accuracy and generalisability. Focus groups, with carefully selected participants sharing common backgrounds, enable dynamic discussions to answer complex questions about motivations and behaviours. Quantitative observation focuses on measurable data, such as size, volume, or frequency, while qualitative observation captures descriptive characteristics from a distance, ensuring that natural behaviours remain unaffected by the presence of the researcher.
This unit's activities included preparation for a seminar: Case Study on Privacy focused on privacy considerations in data collection, where ethical issues often arise. I also submitted my Literature Review Outline and received valuable formative feedback that guided me in refining my research approach. The assigned readings provided a comprehensive understanding of the advantages and limitations of each data collection method, reinforcing the importance of aligning methods with specific research objectives.
A key takeaway from this unit was the realisation that combining multiple methods can produce more comprehensive and reliable research outcomes. For example, integrating qualitative observations with focus groups provides both rich contextual data and insights into participants' perspectives. Similarly, blending case studies with quantitative observation offers a balance between detailed descriptions and objective data measurement. Understanding this nuanced approach has equipped me with essential skills for conducting structured and ethically sound research, fostering a more critical evaluation of methods suited for different types of studies.
